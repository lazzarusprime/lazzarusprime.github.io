What Is a Cavernous Malformation?

A cerebral cavernous malformation (CCM) — also called a cavernoma or cavernous angioma — is an abnormal cluster of blood vessels in the brain or spinal cord. These clusters, sometimes called "mulberry-like" lesions, have thin walls that can leak blood into surrounding tissue.

Unlike typical blood vessels, these malformations lack normal capillary tissue between arteries and veins, making them fragile and prone to bleeding. They can occur anywhere in the central nervous system and range from a few millimeters to several centimeters in size.

CCMs can be sporadic (appearing randomly) or familial (inherited). The familial form follows an autosomal dominant inheritance pattern — meaning one copy of an altered gene is enough to cause the disorder.

Symptoms & Impact
🧠
Headaches — Often the first sign, ranging from mild to severe, sometimes sudden and intense.
⚡
Seizures — One of the most common presentations, occurring in up to 50% of symptomatic patients.
🦾
Weakness or numbness — Neurological deficits depending on lesion location in the brain or spinal cord.
👁️
Vision / balance issues — Brainstem lesions can cause double vision, balance problems, and facial weakness.
🩸
Bleeding episodes — Each bleed can cause new or worsening neurological symptoms depending on where it occurs.
😔
Invisible illness — Many people look completely healthy on the outside while managing serious neurological challenges daily.
Treatment & Research

Currently, there is no medication that can cure or shrink cavernous malformations. Treatment options are limited:

  • Observation — For asymptomatic cases, watchful waiting with regular MRI scans is common.
  • Surgery — Surgical resection is considered when lesions are accessible and causing significant symptoms.
  • Stereotactic radiosurgery — Sometimes used for deep or surgically inaccessible lesions, though effectiveness is debated.
  • Clinical trials — Researchers are actively investigating drug therapies, including some that show early promise in reducing lesion activity.

Research funding is critical. Organizations like the Cavernoma Alliance UK and the Angioma Alliance are leading the charge. Every dollar donated helps move treatments closer to reality.
